# Timeline of the Century of Humiliation (1839-1949)

This timeline provides a chronological overview of major events during China's Century of Humiliation, from the First Opium War to the establishment of the People's Republic of China.

---

## 1839-1842: First Opium War (第一次鸦片战争)

**Background**: British merchants, supported by their government, smuggled opium into China, causing widespread addiction and silver outflow. Chinese authorities attempted to stop the trade.

### Key Events:
- **1839**: Commissioner Lin Zexu destroys opium stocks in Canton (Guangzhou), triggering British military response
- **1840**: British naval forces attack Chinese coastal cities
- **1842**: Treaty of Nanking signed - China's first unequal treaty

**Consequences**:
- Hong Kong ceded to Britain
- Five treaty ports opened to foreign trade
- China forced to pay 21 million silver dollars indemnity
- Extraterritoriality granted to British citizens

---

## 1856-1860: Second Opium War (Arrow War)

**Background**: Western powers sought to expand their privileges in China. The Arrow Incident provided a pretext for renewed conflict.

### Key Events:
- **1856**: Arrow Incident - British and French forces attack China
- **1858**: Treaties of Tianjin signed with Britain, France, Russia, and USA
- **1860**: Anglo-French forces occupy Beijing; Summer Palace (Yuanmingyuan) looted and burned
- **1860**: Convention of Peking signed

**Consequences**:
- Kowloon Peninsula ceded to Britain
- 11 more treaty ports opened
- Foreign legations established in Beijing
- Legalization of opium trade
- Freedom for Christian missionary activities
- Russia acquired territories north of Amur River and east of Ussuri River

---

## 1894-1895: First Sino-Japanese War (甲午战争)

**Background**: Competition over influence in Korea led to war between China and Japan, revealing China's military weakness despite modernization efforts.

### Key Events:
- **1894 July**: War begins with naval battles in Korean waters
- **1894 September**: Battle of Pyongyang - Japanese victory
- **1894 September**: Battle of the Yalu River - Chinese Beiyang Fleet defeated
- **1895 February**: Fall of Weihaiwei - destruction of Beiyang Fleet
- **1895 April**: Treaty of Shimonoseki signed

**Consequences**:
- Taiwan and Penghu Islands ceded to Japan
- Liaodong Peninsula temporarily ceded (later returned after Triple Intervention)
- Korea's independence recognized (ending Chinese suzerainty)
- 200 million taels of silver indemnity
- Four more treaty ports opened
- Japan gained right to establish factories in treaty ports

---

## 1898: Scramble for Concessions

**Background**: Following China's defeat by Japan, European powers rushed to secure territorial and economic concessions.

### Key Concessions:
- **Germany**: Jiaozhou Bay (Qingdao) - 99-year lease
- **Russia**: Liaodong Peninsula (Port Arthur and Dalian) - 25-year lease
- **Britain**: New Territories (Hong Kong) - 99-year lease; Weihaiwei - 25-year lease
- **France**: Guangzhouwan (Zhanjiang) - 99-year lease
- **Various powers**: Railway and mining rights across China

**Impact**: China faced potential partition; "spheres of influence" established by foreign powers.

---

## 1899-1901: Boxer Rebellion and Eight-Nation Alliance

**Background**: Anti-foreign and anti-Christian sentiment erupted in the Boxer movement. The Qing government initially supported the Boxers.

### Key Events:
- **1899**: Boxer movement gains strength in northern China
- **1900 June**: Boxers enter Beijing; foreign legations besieged
- **1900 June**: Qing court declares war on foreign powers
- **1900 August**: Eight-Nation Alliance (Japan, Russia, Britain, France, USA, Germany, Italy, Austria-Hungary) captures Beijing
- **1901 September**: Boxer Protocol signed

**Consequences**:
- 450 million taels of silver indemnity (largest in Chinese history)
- Foreign troops stationed in Beijing
- Execution of pro-Boxer officials
- Destruction of Chinese forts
- Suspension of civil service examinations in affected areas
- Further erosion of Chinese sovereignty

---

## 1911: Xinhai Revolution

**Background**: Decades of humiliation, failed reforms, and Qing incompetence led to revolutionary movements.

### Key Events:
- **1911 October 10**: Wuchang Uprising triggers revolution
- **1912 January 1**: Republic of China established; Sun Yat-sen becomes provisional president
- **1912 February 12**: Last Emperor Puyi abdicates; Qing Dynasty ends

**Significance**: End of imperial China, but political instability continued.

---

## 1915: Twenty-One Demands

**Background**: During World War I, Japan sought to expand its control over China while European powers were distracted.

### Key Demands:
- Recognition of Japanese control over Shandong
- Extension of Japanese leases in Manchuria
- Joint Sino-Japanese control of major Chinese industries
- China not to cede coastal territory to other powers
- Japanese advisors in Chinese government (rejected)

**Consequences**:
- Most demands accepted under pressure
- Sparked May Fourth Movement (1919)
- Intensified Chinese nationalism

---

## 1919: May Fourth Movement

**Background**: Treaty of Versailles transferred German concessions in Shandong to Japan rather than returning them to China.

### Key Events:
- **1919 May 4**: Student protests in Beijing against Versailles Treaty
- Nationwide strikes and boycotts of Japanese goods
- Intellectual and cultural revolution

**Significance**: 
- Birth of modern Chinese nationalism
- Rejection of traditional culture; embrace of science and democracy
- Led to founding of Chinese Communist Party (1921)

---

## 1931-1945: Japanese Invasion and World War II

### 1931: Mukden Incident and Manchuria Occupation
- **1931 September 18**: Japanese stage incident to justify invasion
- Manchuria occupied; puppet state of Manchukuo established (1932)

### 1937-1945: Second Sino-Japanese War
- **1937 July 7**: Marco Polo Bridge Incident - full-scale war begins
- **1937 December**: Nanjing Massacre - estimated 200,000-300,000 civilians killed
- **1938-1945**: Prolonged resistance; China becomes part of Allied powers in WWII
- **1945 August**: Japan surrenders; China regains lost territories

**Impact**:
- Estimated 15-20 million Chinese casualties
- Massive destruction of infrastructure
- Deepened national trauma
- Weakened both Nationalist and Communist forces, setting stage for civil war

---

## 1945-1949: Chinese Civil War

**Background**: After Japanese defeat, conflict resumed between Nationalists (KMT) and Communists (CPC).

### Key Events:
- **1945-1946**: Failed peace negotiations
- **1946-1949**: Full-scale civil war
- **1949 October 1**: People's Republic of China proclaimed in Beijing
- **1949 December**: Nationalist government retreats to Taiwan

**Significance**: End of the Century of Humiliation; beginning of new era in Chinese history.

---

## Historical Significance

The Century of Humiliation profoundly shaped modern China:

1. **National Identity**: Created strong sense of Chinese nationalism and determination to restore national dignity
2. **Political Development**: Led to end of imperial system and search for new political models
3. **Foreign Policy**: Influenced China's approach to international relations and sovereignty
4. **Modernization**: Sparked debates about tradition vs. modernization that continue today
5. **Historical Memory**: Remains central to Chinese historical consciousness and education
