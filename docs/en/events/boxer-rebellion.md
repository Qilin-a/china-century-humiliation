# Boxer Rebellion and Eight-Nation Alliance (1899-1901)

The Boxer Rebellion was an anti-foreign, anti-Christian uprising in China that led to intervention by eight foreign powers. The subsequent Boxer Protocol imposed the most severe terms yet on China, representing one of the lowest points of the Century of Humiliation.

---

## Background

### Origins of the Boxer Movement

**The Society of Righteous and Harmonious Fists (义和拳)**:

- Popular name: "Boxers" (from martial arts practice)
- Origins in Shandong Province (late 1890s)
- Combination of martial arts, spirit possession rituals, and folk religion
- Believed they possessed magical powers and were invulnerable to bullets
- Slogan: "Support the Qing, destroy the foreigners" (扶清灭洋)

**Social and Economic Causes**:

- Natural disasters: Droughts and floods in North China (1898-1900)
- Economic hardship and famine
- Unemployment due to foreign competition
- Resentment of foreign economic privileges
- Disruption of traditional society

**Anti-Foreign Sentiment**:

- Anger at territorial concessions after Sino-Japanese War
- Resentment of Christian missionaries and converts
- Foreign control of railways and mines
- Extraterritoriality and unequal treaties
- Perception of foreign cultural imperialism

### Growth of the Movement

**Initial Phase (1898-1899)**:

- Started as anti-Qing secret society
- Gradually shifted to anti-foreign focus
- Spread from Shandong to Zhili (Hebei) Province
- Attacks on Chinese Christians and missionaries
- Destruction of foreign property (railways, telegraph lines)

**Qing Government Response**:

- Initially attempted to suppress Boxers
- Conservative officials sympathetic to anti-foreign message
- Empress Dowager Cixi saw opportunity to use Boxers against foreigners
- January 1900: Cixi issued edict legitimizing Boxers
- Government shifted from suppression to support

---

## The Crisis Escalates (Spring-Summer 1900)

### Boxer Activities

**May-June 1900**:

- Boxers entered Beijing and Tianjin in large numbers
- Attacks on Chinese Christians intensified
- Foreign property destroyed
- Railways and telegraph lines cut
- Foreign legations in Beijing threatened

**Violence Against Christians**:

- Thousands of Chinese Christians killed
- Foreign missionaries murdered
- Churches burned
- Particularly severe in Shanxi Province under Governor Yuxian
- Estimated 30,000-50,000 Chinese Christians killed nationwide

### Foreign Response

**Military Buildup**:

- Foreign powers sent naval forces to Dagu (Taku) forts
- Marines dispatched to protect legations in Beijing
- May 31: 400+ foreign troops arrived in Beijing
- June 10: Admiral Seymour's relief expedition departed Tianjin (2,000 troops)
- Seymour's force blocked by Boxers and had to retreat

**Siege of the Legations**:

- June 20, 1900: German minister Baron von Ketteler assassinated in Beijing
- Same day: Qing court declared war on foreign powers
- June 20-August 14: Siege of Beijing Legation Quarter
- About 900 foreign civilians and soldiers besieged
- Also 3,000+ Chinese Christians took refuge in legations
- Defended perimeter of about 3/4 mile

## Eight-Nation Alliance Intervention

### Formation of Alliance

**Participating Nations**:

1. Japan (largest contingent: ~20,000 troops)
2. Russia (~13,000 troops)
3. Britain (~12,000 troops)
4. France (~3,000 troops)
5. United States (~3,000 troops)
6. Germany (~900 troops initially, more later)
7. Austria-Hungary (~300 troops)
8. Italy (~80 troops)

**Total Force**: Approximately 55,000 troops

### Military Campaign

**Capture of Dagu Forts (June 17, 1900)**:

- Allied naval forces attacked and captured forts
- Opened way for landing of troops
- Qing court viewed this as act of war

**Battle of Tianjin (July 13-14, 1900)**:

- Allied forces captured Tianjin after fierce fighting
- City looted and burned
- Established base for march on Beijing

**March to Beijing (August 1900)**:

- Allied force of ~20,000 departed Tianjin (August 4)
- Fought several engagements with Qing and Boxer forces
- Battle of Beicang (August 5)
- Relatively light resistance due to poor Qing military organization

**Relief of Beijing (August 14, 1900)**:

- Allied forces entered Beijing
- Legation Quarter relieved after 55-day siege
- Empress Dowager Cixi and Emperor Guangxu fled to Xi'an
- Allied troops occupied Beijing

### Atrocities and Looting

**Allied Conduct**:

- Widespread looting of Beijing
- Imperial Palace (Forbidden City) plundered
- Destruction of cultural artifacts
- Killings of Chinese civilians
- Rape and violence against population
- Punitive expeditions to surrounding areas

**German "Hunnenrede" (Hun Speech)**:

- Kaiser Wilhelm II's infamous speech to German troops
- Urged troops to be merciless like "Huns under Attila"
- "No quarter will be given, no prisoners will be taken"
- Contributed to brutal conduct of campaign

**Scope of Destruction**:

- Estimates of Chinese deaths: 50,000-100,000+
- Many villages destroyed in punitive expeditions
- Cultural treasures stolen or destroyed
- Economic devastation of affected regions

## Boxer Protocol (辛丑条约, September 7, 1901)

### Negotiations

**Chinese Negotiators**:

- Li Hongzhang (died during negotiations, November 1900)
- Prince Qing (Yikuang)

**Foreign Powers**: Representatives of all eight nations plus Spain, Belgium, Netherlands

### Key Provisions

**1. Indemnity (赔款)**:

- **450 million taels of silver** (about 18,000 tons)
- Equivalent to ~$333 million USD
- To be paid over 39 years with 4% annual interest
- **Total with interest: 982 million taels**
- Largest indemnity in Chinese history
- Secured by salt tax and maritime customs revenues
- Per capita: approximately 1 tael per Chinese person

**2. Punishment of Officials**:

- Execution of 10 high officials who supported Boxers
- 120+ officials punished with death, exile, or dismissal
- Governor Yuxian of Shanxi executed for killing missionaries
- Posthumous degradation of officials
- Prohibition of civil service examinations in 45 cities for 5 years

**3. Military Provisions**:

- Destruction of Dagu forts and other fortifications
- Foreign troops stationed in Beijing (Legation Guard)
- Foreign military presence along railway from Beijing to sea
- Prohibition on importing arms for 2 years
- Permanent foreign military presence in Chinese capital

**4. Legation Quarter**:

- Exclusive foreign control of Legation Quarter in Beijing
- Chinese not allowed to reside in quarter
- Fortifications allowed
- Effectively foreign territory in Chinese capital

**5. Diplomatic**:

- Formal apologies and expiatory missions to Germany and Japan
- Erection of monuments to killed foreigners
- Suspension of official examinations where foreigners killed

**6. Anti-Foreign Organizations**:

- Death penalty for membership in anti-foreign societies
- Officials held responsible for anti-foreign violence in their jurisdictions
- Strict prohibition of anti-foreign activities

### Distribution of Indemnity

**Shares by Country**:

- Russia: 28.97%
- Germany: 20.02%
- France: 15.75%
- Britain: 11.25%
- Japan: 7.73%
- United States: 7.32%
- Italy: 5.91%
- Belgium: 1.89%
- Austria-Hungary: 0.89%
- Others: 0.27%

## Consequences

### For China

**Financial Catastrophe**:

- Indemnity equivalent to 2-3 years of government revenue
- Required massive tax increases
- Forced dependence on foreign loans
- Customs and salt revenues mortgaged to foreign powers
- Crippled government finances for decades

**Loss of Sovereignty**:

- Foreign troops permanently stationed in capital
- Legation Quarter under foreign control
- Foreign control of customs revenues
- Unable to import arms
- Effective semi-colonial status

**Political Impact**:

- Demonstrated Qing Dynasty's complete inability to resist foreign powers
- Discredited conservative faction
- Led to belated reform efforts (New Policies, 1901-1911)
- Contributed to revolutionary sentiment
- Hastened fall of Qing Dynasty (1911)

**Social Impact**:

- Deep national humiliation
- Strengthened anti-Manchu sentiment
- Increased support for radical reform or revolution
- Trauma in collective memory

### International Consequences

**U.S. "Open Door" Policy**:

- Secretary of State John Hay's circular notes (1899-1900)
- Advocated equal commercial access for all powers
- Opposed partition of China
- Established U.S. policy framework for China

**Indemnity Returns**:

- **United States** (1908): Returned portion for Chinese student education
  - Established Tsinghua University
  - Boxer Indemnity Scholarship Program
- **Britain** (1922): Returned portion for educational purposes
- **Other countries**: Various partial returns or remissions

**Power Dynamics**:

- Japan emerged as major power in China
- Russia and Japan competed for influence in Manchuria
- Led to Russo-Japanese War (1904-1905)
- Beginning of Japanese dominance in East Asia

## Historical Debates

### Nature of Boxer Movement

**Patriotic Resistance View**:

- Anti-imperialist popular movement
- Legitimate resistance to foreign aggression
- Spontaneous uprising of oppressed people
- Heroic defense of Chinese sovereignty

**Xenophobic Violence View**:

- Irrational, superstitious movement
- Indiscriminate violence against foreigners and Christians
- Manipulated by conservative Qing officials
- Setback for China's modernization

**Balanced Perspective**:

- Complex movement with multiple causes
- Legitimate grievances against imperialism
- Methods included excessive violence
- Both patriotic and xenophobic elements
- Tragic consequences for China

### Qing Government Responsibility

**Arguments for Culpability**:

- Cynically used Boxers for own purposes
- Declaration of war on eight powers was reckless
- Failed to protect foreign diplomats
- Shared responsibility for violence

**Mitigating Factors**:

- Lost control of situation
- Divided court between conservatives and moderates
- Faced impossible situation with no good options
- Some officials protected foreigners

### Foreign Conduct

**Legitimate Defense**:

- Had right to protect diplomats and nationals
- Responded to attacks and siege
- Rescued besieged legations

**Excessive Retaliation**:

- Punitive expeditions killed many civilians
- Widespread looting and destruction
- Excessive indemnity demands
- Collective punishment of Chinese people

## Legacy

### Historical Memory

**In China**:

- Symbol of foreign imperialism and aggression
- Example of national humiliation
- Warning against superstition and backwardness
- Debate over patriotism vs. xenophobia
- Part of patriotic education curriculum

**In the West**:

- Often portrayed as "rescue" of besieged diplomats
- Less emphasis on punitive aspects
- Cultural artifacts in Western museums from this period
- Source of stereotypes about China

### Long-term Impact

**On Chinese Politics**:

- Contributed to fall of Qing Dynasty
- Strengthened revolutionary movements
- Influenced Chinese nationalism
- Shaped attitudes toward foreign intervention

**On International Relations**:

- Demonstrated China's weakness
- Encouraged further foreign encroachment
- Set precedent for collective intervention
- Influenced development of international law

**Cultural Impact**:

- Loss of cultural treasures
- Trauma in national consciousness
- Influenced Chinese views of modernization
- Shaped debates about tradition vs. modernity

---

## Comparison with Other Events

**Similarities to Opium Wars**:

- Foreign military intervention
- Unequal treaty imposed
- Loss of sovereignty
- National humiliation

**Unique Aspects**:

- Popular anti-foreign movement (not just government)
- Eight-nation coalition (unprecedented scale)
- Largest indemnity in Chinese history
- Foreign troops in capital
- Most severe terms of Century of Humiliation

---

## Lessons and Reflections

### For China

1. Xenophobia and superstition cannot defeat modern powers
2. Need for genuine modernization, not just anti-foreignism
3. Importance of rational foreign policy
4. Dangers of manipulating popular movements

### For International Community

1. Collective punishment has long-term consequences
2. Excessive demands create lasting resentment
3. Cultural sensitivity in international relations
4. Balance between protecting nationals and respecting sovereignty

### Universal Lessons

1. Dangers of extreme nationalism and xenophobia
2. Consequences of imperialism and foreign domination
3. Importance of addressing legitimate grievances
4. Need for restraint in use of military force
