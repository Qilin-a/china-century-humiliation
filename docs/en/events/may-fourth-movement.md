# May Fourth Movement (1919)

The May Fourth Movement was a cultural and political movement that emerged in response to China's treatment at the Paris Peace Conference following World War I. It marked a turning point in modern Chinese history, combining anti-imperialist nationalism with a radical cultural revolution that rejected traditional Confucian values.

---

## Background

### World War I and China

**China's Participation**:
- August 1917: China declared war on Germany and Austria-Hungary
- Sent 140,000+ laborers to Europe (Chinese Labor Corps)
- Supported Allied war effort
- Expected fair treatment at peace conference

**Chinese Expectations**:
- Return of German concessions in Shandong Province
- Abolition of unequal treaties
- Recognition as equal nation
- End to foreign privileges in China

### Paris Peace Conference (1919)

**Chinese Delegation**:
- Led by Foreign Minister Lu Zhengxiang
- Presented "Seven Demands":
  1. Abolish spheres of influence
  2. Withdraw foreign troops and police
  3. Abolish extraterritoriality
  4. Return foreign concessions
  5. Return leased territories
  6. Abolish Twenty-One Demands (Japan, 1915)
  7. Return tariff autonomy

**The Shandong Question**:
- Germany had controlled Jiaozhou Bay and Qingdao since 1898
- Japan seized these territories in 1914
- Secret treaties (1917-1918): Allies promised Shandong to Japan
- China demanded direct return of German concessions
- Japan insisted on keeping Shandong

**Versailles Treaty Decision**:
- **April 30, 1919**: Decision announced
- Shandong transferred to Japan, not returned to China
- Chinese demands rejected
- China treated as defeated nation despite being on winning side
- Profound shock and humiliation in China

## The May Fourth Incident (May 4, 1919)

### Student Protests in Beijing

**Immediate Trigger**:
- News of Versailles decision reached Beijing
- Students organized mass protest
- Anger at foreign powers and Chinese government

**May 4, 1919 Demonstration**:
- **3,000+ students** from 13 Beijing universities gathered at Tiananmen
- Peking University students led the protest
- Slogans:
  - "Refuse to sign the Versailles Treaty!"
  - "Abolish the Twenty-One Demands!"
  - "Boycott Japanese goods!"
  - "Punish the traitors!"
  - "Struggle for sovereignty externally, get rid of the national traitors at home!"

**Violence and Arrests**:
- Students marched to Legation Quarter
- Attacked home of Cao Rulin (pro-Japanese official)
- Set fire to his residence
- Beat Zhang Zongxiang (Chinese minister to Japan)
- Police arrested 32 students
- Government crackdown began

### Spread of the Movement

**Nationwide Protests (May-June 1919)**:
- Spread to over 200 cities
- Students in Shanghai, Tianjin, Wuhan, Guangzhou joined
- Workers went on strike
- Merchants closed shops (sanba: "triple suspension")
- Became national movement

**Participants**:
- **Students**: Primary organizers and activists
- **Workers**: Shanghai workers' general strike (June 5)
- **Merchants**: Business strikes and boycotts
- **Intellectuals**: Writers, professors, journalists
- **Women**: Active participation, gender equality demands

**Government Response**:
- Initially repressive: arrests, censorship
- June 3-4: Mass arrests of students (over 1,000)
- Public pressure forced government to back down
- June 10: Released arrested students
- June 28: China refused to sign Versailles Treaty

## New Culture Movement

### Intellectual Revolution

**Origins (1915-1919)**:
- Preceded May Fourth political protests
- Led by intellectuals like Chen Duxiu, Hu Shi, Lu Xun
- Journal "New Youth" (新青年) as platform
- Critique of traditional Chinese culture

**Core Ideas**:
- **Mr. Science** (赛先生): Scientific method and rationalism
- **Mr. Democracy** (德先生): Democratic governance and individual rights
- Rejection of Confucianism as feudal and backward
- Embrace of Western ideas and values
- Literary revolution: vernacular Chinese (baihua) vs. classical Chinese

**Key Figures**:

1. **Chen Duxiu** (陈独秀):
   - Founded "New Youth" magazine (1915)
   - Dean at Peking University
   - Later co-founder of Chinese Communist Party (1921)

2. **Hu Shi** (胡适):
   - Advocated literary revolution
   - Promoted vernacular Chinese
   - Pragmatist philosopher

3. **Lu Xun** (鲁迅):
   - Greatest modern Chinese writer
   - "A Madman's Diary" (1918): first vernacular short story
   - Scathing critique of traditional society

4. **Li Dazhao** (李大钊):
   - Peking University librarian
   - Introduced Marxism to China
   - Co-founder of CCP

### Cultural Transformation

**Literary Revolution**:
- Vernacular Chinese (baihua) replaced classical Chinese in literature
- New forms: modern poetry, short stories, novels
- Accessibility: ordinary people could read and write
- Newspapers and magazines in vernacular

**Social Reforms Advocated**:
- Women's rights and gender equality
- End to arranged marriages
- Abolition of foot-binding
- Educational reform
- Individual freedom
- Questioning of family hierarchy

**Ideological Diversity**:
- Liberalism and democracy
- Anarchism
- Socialism and Marxism
- Pragmatism
- Intense intellectual debates

## Political Consequences

### Immediate Results

**Diplomatic Victory**:
- China refused to sign Versailles Treaty (June 28, 1919)
- Three pro-Japanese officials dismissed:
  - Cao Rulin (Communications Minister)
  - Lu Zongyu (Currency Bureau Director)
  - Zhang Zongxiang (Minister to Japan)
- Symbolic victory for protesters

**Limitations**:
- Shandong still went to Japan
- Unequal treaties remained
- Foreign privileges continued
- Warlord government still weak

### Long-term Political Impact

**Rise of Political Movements**:

1. **Chinese Communist Party (CCP)**:
   - Founded July 1921
   - Many May Fourth activists became communists
   - Chen Duxiu: first CCP General Secretary
   - Li Dazhao: co-founder
   - Mao Zedong: participated in May Fourth activities

2. **Reorganized Kuomintang (KMT)**:
   - Sun Yat-sen reorganized party (1919-1924)
   - Adopted more radical platform
   - First United Front with CCP (1924-1927)

**Shift in Political Thinking**:
- Disillusionment with Western democracy
- Interest in Soviet model
- Emphasis on mass mobilization
- Anti-imperialism became central
- Class struggle concepts introduced

### Nationalism

**New Form of Nationalism**:
- Popular, mass-based (not just elite)
- Anti-imperialist focus
- Cultural confidence alongside modernization
- Student activism as political force
- Boycotts as political weapon

**Anti-Japanese Sentiment**:
- Intensified by Shandong issue
- Boycotts of Japanese goods
- Foundation for later resistance
- Shaped Sino-Japanese relations

- Challenged traditional gender roles

**Feminist Movement**:
- Women's rights became major issue
- Critique of arranged marriage
- Advocacy for education and careers
- Literary works on women's liberation

### Educational Reform

**Changes in Education**:
- Vernacular Chinese in textbooks
- Modern curriculum
- Co-education promoted
- Study abroad encouraged
- Critical thinking emphasized

**Peking University**:
- Center of New Culture Movement
- Chancellor Cai Yuanpei supported reform
- Academic freedom
- Attracted leading intellectuals

### Literary Achievements

**New Literature**:
- Lu Xun's short stories
- Guo Moruo's poetry
- Ba Jin's novels
- Realistic depiction of society
- Social criticism

**Journals and Publications**:
- "New Youth" (新青年)
- "New Tide" (新潮)

## Historical Significance

### Turning Point in Modern China

**Political**:
- Marked beginning of mass politics
- Students as political force
- Foundation for later movements
- Contributed to CCP founding

**Cultural**:
- Break with traditional culture
- Modernization of language and literature
- Intellectual liberation
- Questioning of authority

**Social**:
- Women's emancipation movement
- Individual rights consciousness
- Social mobility
- Urban-rural divide

### Comparison with Other Movements

**Similar to**:
- European Enlightenment (rationalism, science)
- French Revolution (radical cultural change)
- Russian Revolution (political radicalism)

**Unique Aspects**:
- Combined anti-imperialism with cultural revolution
- Student-led mass movement
- Vernacular language revolution
- Synthesis of nationalism and internationalism

## Debates and Interpretations

### In Mainland China (PRC)

**Official Narrative**:
- Patriotic, anti-imperialist movement
- Beginning of New Democratic Revolution
- Prepared conditions for CCP founding
- Part of world proletarian revolution
- May 4 celebrated as "Youth Day"

**Emphasis**:
- Anti-imperialism and anti-feudalism
- Mass mobilization
- Revolutionary potential
- Link to Communist movement

### In Taiwan (ROC)

**Alternative View**:
- Cultural renaissance
- Democratic and liberal ideals
- Connection to Sun Yat-sen's principles
- Modernization movement
- Less emphasis on class struggle

### Western Scholarship

**Academic Perspectives**:
- Complex, multifaceted movement
- Both progressive and problematic aspects
- Rejection of tradition had costs
- Contributed to political radicalization
- Cultural iconoclasm excessive

**Debates**:
- Was rejection of tradition necessary?
- Did it prepare ground for totalitarianism?
- Balance between tradition and modernity
- Role of intellectuals in politics

## Conclusion

The May Fourth Movement was a watershed in modern Chinese history, combining anti-imperialist nationalism with radical cultural transformation. It emerged from China's humiliation at Versailles but grew into a comprehensive challenge to traditional society and culture.

The movement's dual nature—political protest and cultural revolution—made it uniquely influential. It mobilized students, workers, and merchants in unprecedented ways, established new forms of political activism, and fundamentally transformed Chinese language and literature.

While the movement achieved limited immediate political success, its long-term impact was profound. It contributed to the founding of the Chinese Communist Party, energized Chinese nationalism, modernized Chinese culture, and established patterns of student activism that continue to this day.

The May Fourth Movement represents both the culmination of China's response to the Century of Humiliation and the beginning of new revolutionary movements that would eventually transform China. Its legacy remains contested but undeniably central to understanding modern China.
