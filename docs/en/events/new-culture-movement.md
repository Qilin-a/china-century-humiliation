# New Culture Movement (1915-1920s)

The New Culture Movement (新文化运动, Xin Wenhua Yundong) was a revolutionary intellectual and cultural movement in China that challenged traditional Confucian values and promoted modernization, science, democracy, and individualism. Beginning around 1915 and reaching its peak during the May Fourth Movement of 1919, it fundamentally transformed Chinese intellectual life and laid the groundwork for political and social revolutions that would reshape 20th-century China.

## Key Figures and Leaders

### Chen Duxiu (1879-1942)

**Founder and Leader**:
- Founded *New Youth* magazine (1915)
- Most influential voice of movement
- Advocate of democracy and science
- Later co-founder of Chinese Communist Party (1921)
- Imprisoned and expelled, died in poverty

**Key Ideas**:
- Total rejection of Confucianism
- Embrace of Western democracy
- Scientific worldview
- Individual liberation
- Revolutionary transformation

### Hu Shi (1891-1962)

**Pragmatist and Reformer**:
- Studied under John Dewey at Columbia
- Advocated literary revolution
- Promoted vernacular Chinese (baihua)
- Emphasized gradual reform
- Later opposed communism

**Literary Contributions**:
- "A Preliminary Discussion of Literary Reform" (1917)
- Promoted vernacular literature
- Scholarly and methodical approach
- Bridge between radicalism and moderation
- Influential educator

### Lu Xun (1881-1936)

**Greatest Modern Chinese Writer**:
- Author of "A Madman's Diary" (1918)
- "The True Story of Ah Q" (1921)
- Devastating critique of traditional society
- Master of satire and irony
- Cultural icon

**Literary Impact**:
- Vernacular short stories
- Psychological realism
- Social criticism
- Influence on modern Chinese literature
- Symbol of intellectual courage

### Li Dazhao (1889-1927)

**Marxist Pioneer**:
- Librarian at Peking University
- Introduced Marxism to China
- Influenced Mao Zedong
- Co-founder of Chinese Communist Party
- Executed by warlord (1927)

**Ideas**:
- Socialism and Marxism
- Rural revolution potential
- Critique of capitalism
- Anti-imperialism
- Worker-peasant alliance

### Cai Yuanpei (1868-1940)

**Educational Reformer**:
- Chancellor of Peking University (1917-1927)
- Promoted academic freedom
- "Thought without boundaries"
- Aesthetic education
- Modernized Chinese education

**Contributions**:
- Transformed Peking University into intellectual center
- Hired Chen Duxiu, Hu Shi, Li Dazhao
- Protected radical students and faculty
- Promoted freedom of expression
- Balanced various ideological currents

## Conclusion

The New Culture Movement was one of the most profound intellectual and cultural transformations in Chinese history. It fundamentally challenged traditional Confucian civilization and sought to create a new culture based on science, democracy, and individual liberation. While it did not achieve all its goals and went to extremes in rejecting tradition, it permanently transformed Chinese intellectual life and culture.

The movement's promotion of vernacular Chinese democratized literature and education, making them accessible to broader populations. Its critique of traditional ethics, especially regarding women and youth, opened space for social transformation. Its introduction of Western ideas and critical thinking created modern Chinese intellectuals who would lead various political movements.

Most significantly, the New Culture Movement prepared the ground for China's 20th-century revolutions. It radicalized a generation of intellectuals and students, many of whom became founders of the Chinese Communist Party. It created the ideological and cultural conditions for mass political movements. The May Fourth Movement of 1919, growing out of New Culture, marked the beginning of modern Chinese revolutionary politics.

The legacy of the New Culture Movement remains contested and complex. It represents both cultural enlightenment and iconoclastic excess, individual liberation and social fragmentation, modernization and identity crisis. Understanding this movement is essential for comprehending modern Chinese intellectual history and the cultural foundations of China's revolutionary transformations.
